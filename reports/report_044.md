## II. Carbon Slider Technical Specifications and Replacement Parameters

Understanding the technical specifications of carbon sliders is essential for accurate consumption estimation, as dimensions, materials, and wear characteristics directly determine replacement frequency.

### 2.1 What is a Carbon Slider?

A carbon slider (碳滑板, also called carbon contact strip or pantograph collector strip) is the current-collecting component mounted on a railway pantograph that maintains sliding electrical contact with the overhead catenary wire.

**Functional Principle**: Carbon sliders enable current collection WITHOUT excessive catenary wear BECAUSE the carbon material is designed to be sacrificial - it wears preferentially compared to the copper contact wire. This matters BECAUSE replacing contact wire across an entire metro line costs orders of magnitude more than replacing carbon sliders on vehicles. As a result, the carbon slider functions as a designed wear component protecting expensive infrastructure.

**Key Components:**
1. **Carbon contact strip** - Primary current-carrying and wearing element
2. **Metal backing/holder** - Structural support and electrical connection
3. **Spring suspension** - Maintains contact pressure (70-120 N typical)
4. **Mounting hardware** - Secures assembly to pantograph frame

### 2.2 Material Types

Carbon sliders for urban rail applications utilize three primary material categories, each with different performance and cost profiles:

#### 2.2.1 Metal-Impregnated Carbon (浸金属碳)

Metal-impregnated carbon sliders incorporate copper, silver, or other metal particles within the carbon matrix.

| Property | Typical Value | Application |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Electrical resistivity | 8-15 μΩ·m | High-current metro |
| Current capacity | 2,500-4,000 A | Mainline operations |
| Copper content | 15-35% by volume | Varies by grade |
| Price premium | +30-50% vs pure carbon | Premium segment |

**Performance Mechanism**: Metal inclusions enhance electrical conductivity (reducing contact resistance by 30-50%) while the carbon matrix provides wear resistance. This matters BECAUSE lower contact resistance reduces electrical heating and arcing at the contact interface. As a result, metal-impregnated variants are preferred for high-current metro applications.

#### 2.2.2 Pure Carbon/Graphite (纯碳)

Pure carbon sliders use carbon or graphite materials without metal impregnation.

| Property | Typical Value | Application |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Electrical resistivity | 15-30 μΩ·m | Light rail, trams |
| Current capacity | 1,000-2,000 A | Lower-current systems |
| Lubrication properties | Superior | Wire protection priority |
| Cost | Baseline | Standard applications |

**Performance Mechanism**: Graphite's layered crystalline structure provides natural lubrication properties that reduce mechanical friction and contact wire wear. This matters for operators prioritizing infrastructure preservation over maximum current capacity.

#### 2.2.3 Composite Materials (复合材料)

Modern composite carbon sliders incorporate advanced materials such as carbon fiber reinforcement, ceramic particles, or engineered polymer binders.

| Property | Typical Value | Application |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Service life | +20-40% vs standard | Extended life needs |
| Environmental resistance | Enhanced | Harsh conditions |
| Cost | +50-100% vs standard | Premium segment |
| Technology status | Growing adoption | Newer systems |

**Performance Mechanism**: Composites can be engineered to optimize multiple parameters simultaneously. This matters BECAUSE metro systems face diverse operating conditions. These materials represent the technological frontier, offering extended service life at higher unit cost.

### 2.3 Dimensional Specifications

Carbon slider dimensions for Chinese urban rail applications conform to the **GB/T 38955-2020** national standard for urban rail transit carbon sliders:

**Standard Dimensional Ranges:**

| Parameter | Urban Rail (Metro) | Light Rail/Tram | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Contact length | 900-1,400 mm | 800-1,000 mm | Full pantograph width |
| Contact width | 25-40 mm | 20-35 mm | Contact patch size |
| Initial thickness | 20-28 mm | 18-25 mm | New component |
| Wear limit thickness | 5-8 mm | 5-7 mm | Replacement threshold |
| Usable wear depth | 12-22 mm | 12-18 mm | Service life material |

**Dimensional Rationale**: Contact length spans full pantograph width to maintain contact under wire lateral movement (±400-600mm zigzag pattern). Contact width balances current distribution against friction. Thickness determines replacement interval - thicker sliders cost more but last longer.

Carbon sliders are replaced based on wear depth reaching defined thresholds:

### 2.7 Factors Affecting Wear Rate

Multiple factors influence carbon slider consumption rates:

**Metro-Specific Wear Acceleration**: Urban metro systems experience higher per-km wear than mainline rail BECAUSE frequent station stops (every 1-2 km) create current transients during acceleration that cause disproportionate electrical erosion. The market size estimation employs a **fleet-based consumption model** rather than route-length based approaches BECAUSE carbon slider wear is a function of vehicle operating mileage and electrical current collection duty cycles, which correlate directly with vehicle counts rather than network route kilometers.

**Primary Calculation Formula:**

Annual Consumption = (Fleet Size × Sliders per Vehicle) ÷ Replacement Cycle (years)

Annual Consumption = (Fleet Size × Sliders per Vehicle × Annual km/Vehicle) ÷ Slider Lifetime (km)
