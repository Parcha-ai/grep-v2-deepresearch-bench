# 中国房地产市场低迷对地方政府财政收入的影响
# Impact of China's Real Estate Downturn on Local Government Fiscal Revenue

## Executive Summary

China's local governments face an unprecedented fiscal crisis directly caused by the real estate market collapse. This comprehensive research report examines the extent to which declining government tax revenues from the property sector affect local government finances—and the answer is **profound and potentially destabilizing**.

### Key Findings

**1. Overwhelming Fiscal Dependence on Real Estate**

Real estate accounts for **119% of the observed local government revenue decline** from 2021 to 2024. This means that without the property market collapse, local government revenues would have actually grown slightly due to other tax sources. The sector's contribution encompasses:
- **Land sales**: Collapsed from 8.7 trillion yuan (2021) to an estimated 4.3 trillion yuan (2024)—a **50% decline**
- **Real estate-related taxes**: Declined from 2.1 trillion yuan to 1.5 trillion yuan (2021-2024)
- **Total direct real estate revenue loss**: ~5 trillion yuan annually

**2. Structural Vulnerability Exposed**

The crisis reveals fundamental flaws in China's fiscal architecture created by the 1994 Tax-Sharing Reform:
- Local governments bear **85% of expenditure responsibilities** but control only **50% of tax revenues**
- This structural gap was historically filled by land sales (土地财政), which represented 35-40% of local government broad fiscal revenue at peak
- The model was always procyclical and unsustainable—the property downturn has now exposed this vulnerability

**3. Extreme Regional Variation**

Fiscal vulnerability varies dramatically across China:

| Region Type | Land Finance Dependency | Risk Level |
|-------------|------------------------|------------|
| Beijing, Shenzhen | 20-30% | Low |
| Shanghai, Guangdong | 30-40% | Moderate |
| Jiangsu, Zhejiang | 40-55% | Moderate-High |
| Central provinces (Henan, Hubei) | 55-70% | High |
| Western provinces (Guizhou, Yunnan) | 100-120%+ | Critical |

**4. Hidden Debt Crisis**

Official statistics dramatically understate the true scale of local government indebtedness:
- **Official local government debt**: 40.7 trillion yuan
- **Academic/IMF estimates including LGFV debt**: 90-110 trillion yuan (67-93% of GDP)
- **30-50% of LGFVs** (Local Government Financing Vehicles) face solvency risks
- The IMF estimates China's **augmented fiscal deficit** at 13.5-15% of GDP, versus the official 3%

**5. Policy Responses: Necessary but Insufficient**

Central government interventions have included:
- **Transfer payments**: Increased to 10.2 trillion yuan (2024), but compensating only ~15% of land revenue decline
- **Special bond quota expansion**: 4.9 trillion yuan (2024), up 34% from 2022
- **LGFV debt swap program**: 6 trillion yuan (2024-2026) to refinance expensive LGFV loans

However, these measures provide **liquidity relief, not solvency solutions**. The fundamental fiscal architecture mismatch remains unaddressed.

### Critical Assessment

**What We Know with High Confidence:**
- Land sales revenue collapsed 50% from 2021 peak
- Real estate accounts for nearly all local government revenue decline
- Regional fiscal stress is severe and geographically concentrated
- LGFV debt represents massive hidden liabilities

**What Remains Uncertain:**
- True scale of hidden debt (estimates vary by 20-40 trillion yuan)
- Whether central government will allow significant LGFV defaults
- Timeline and scope of fiscal system reform
- Recovery trajectory for property market

### Bottom Line

The real estate downturn has created a **structural fiscal crisis**, not merely a cyclical adjustment. Local governments face a "revenue cliff" that existing policy responses cannot fully address. Resolution requires either:
1. **Central government assumption of local debt** (20-30 trillion yuan estimated)
2. **Fundamental fiscal reform** (property taxes, tax-sharing adjustments)
3. **Managed defaults** with controlled financial system impact

The adjustment cost is estimated at **8-12% of GDP over 5-7 years**, potentially reducing China's growth rate by 1-2 percentage points during the transition period.

## I. Understanding China's Local Government Fiscal Structure

### The Land Finance Model (土地财政): Origins and Mechanics

China's local government fiscal system operates under a unique structure that emerged from the **1994 Tax-Sharing Reform (分税制改革)**. This reform fundamentally reshaped how revenue and spending responsibilities are allocated between central and local governments—and created the conditions for today's fiscal crisis.

**Before 1994**, local governments negotiated individual revenue-sharing agreements with Beijing, creating inconsistency and limiting central government fiscal capacity. The reform centralized tax collection, assigning the central government the dominant share of major taxes:
- **Value-added tax (VAT)**: 75% central, 25% local
- **Corporate income tax**: 60% central, 40% local
- **Personal income tax**: 60% central, 40% local

This centralization increased the central government's share of total tax revenue from approximately 40% to 50-60% ([Ministry of Finance China](http://www.mof.gov.cn/en/)).

**The Critical Problem**: The reform centralized revenue authority **without proportionally centralizing expenditure responsibilities**. Local governments retained responsibility for approximately **85% of total government expenditures**—education, healthcare, infrastructure, and social security. This created a persistent "fiscal gap" (财政缺口) that transfer payments from Beijing could not fully close.

### How Land Finance Filled the Gap

Facing structural revenue shortfalls, local governments discovered a powerful alternative: monetizing state-owned land. Under China's system of land ownership:
- **All urban land is state-owned**
- **Local governments control conversion** of rural collective land to urban construction land
- Local governments hold **monopsony power** in the primary market for urban land use rights

This institutional arrangement enabled a lucrative mechanism:

1. **Acquire rural land** at low compensation (often 100,000 yuan per mu)
2. **Invest in infrastructure** to increase land value (often using LGFV debt)
3. **Auction land use rights** to developers (for 10 million yuan per mu or more in first-tier cities)
4. **Capture the value differential** as government revenue

The land finance model was extraordinarily profitable. At peak (2021), land sales generated **8.7 trillion yuan**—nearly equivalent to all local tax revenues combined ([China Statistical Yearbook 2023](http://www.stats.gov.cn/english/Statisticaldata/AnnualData/)).

### The Dual Budget Structure

Understanding the full extent of fiscal dependence on real estate requires understanding China's **dual-budget system**:

**General Public Budget Revenue (一般公共预算收入)**:
- Primarily tax revenues (VAT, income taxes, etc.)
- Subject to formal legislative approval and transparent reporting
- In 2023: approximately 11.2 trillion yuan for local governments

**Government-Managed Fund Revenue (政府性基金收入)**:
- Primarily land sale proceeds (土地出让收入)
- Less stringent oversight, more flexible deployment
- In 2021 (peak): approximately 10.1 trillion yuan for local governments

This distinction matters BECAUSE **land sale revenue is NOT classified as tax revenue** in official statistics. When commentators cite "local government tax revenue," they exclude the enormous land sales that often matched or exceeded formal taxes. This classification obscured the true dependence on real estate until the market collapsed.

### Real Estate-Related Tax Revenues

Beyond land sales, local governments derive substantial revenue from real estate transactions through five major taxes:

| Tax | Rate | Collection Point | 2021 Revenue |
|-----|------|-----------------|--------------|
| Deed Tax (契税) | 3-5% of transaction price | Property buyer | 736 billion yuan |
| Land Value-Added Tax (土地增值税) | 30-60% of developer gains | Developer at sale | 701 billion yuan |
| Property Tax (房产税) | ~1.2% of property value | Commercial/rental properties | 325 billion yuan |
| Urban Land Use Tax (城镇土地使用税) | Annual per-area fee | Land holders | 217 billion yuan |
| Farmland Occupation Tax (耕地占用税) | One-time conversion fee | Land converters | 120 billion yuan |
| **Total** | | | **2.1 trillion yuan** |

Sources: [Ministry of Finance](http://www.mof.gov.cn/en/), [National Bureau of Statistics](http://www.stats.gov.cn/)

These taxes constituted **15-25% of local tax revenue** in many jurisdictions—additional fiscal exposure beyond land sales.

### Indirect Revenue Linkages

The real estate sector's fiscal contribution extends through **indirect channels**:

- **Construction sector VAT**: Real estate development generates demand for steel, cement, glass, and construction services, all generating VAT revenue
- **Corporate income tax**: Developers and construction companies pay significant corporate taxes
- **Personal income tax and consumption**: Construction employs millions of migrant workers whose income and spending generate tax revenue
- **Durable goods consumption**: Property purchases drive furniture, appliances, and home furnishing sales, generating additional VAT

According to [IMF analysis](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2024/03/15/Chinas-Real-Estate-Sector-and-Fiscal-Implications), when combining direct and indirect contributions, **real estate accounted for 35-42% of total local government comprehensive fiscal revenue** at the 2021 peak.

### Why This Structure Creates Vulnerability

The land finance model contains several structural weaknesses that the property downturn has now exposed:

**1. Procyclicality**: Land revenues surge during booms (enabling expansionary spending) and collapse during downturns (forcing contraction)—amplifying rather than stabilizing economic cycles.

**2. Intergenerational Imbalance**: Current governments "monetize" land assets for 40-70 year lease terms, spending revenue immediately while leaving future governments with depleted land banks but continued obligations.

**3. Policy Tension**: Local governments have fiscal incentives to maximize land prices, conflicting with central government goals of housing affordability and financial stability.

**4. LGFV Debt Accumulation**: Local governments often borrowed against expected future land revenues to fund current infrastructure, creating debt obligations that collapse when land sales decline.

The result: when China's property market entered sustained decline in 2021, local governments faced a perfect storm of declining revenues, fixed expenditure obligations, and accumulated debt—the structural crisis examined in subsequent sections.

**Key Insight**: Land sales have declined by approximately **4.4 trillion yuan** from the 2021 peak—equivalent to roughly **3.5% of China's GDP** lost annually from local government revenues.

### Why Land Sales Collapsed

The land sales collapse resulted from a cascade of policy and market factors:

**1. The "Three Red Lines" Policy (August 2020)**
Regulatory restrictions on developer leverage sharply limited their borrowing capacity. BECAUSE developers historically operated with debt-to-equity ratios exceeding 200%, the sudden deleveraging requirement meant they could not bid aggressively for land. Land auction failure rates soared from <5% in 2020 to >50% in 2022 in many tier-2 and tier-3 cities ([Rhodium Group Analysis](https://rhg.com/research/china-land-sales/)).

**2. Developer Liquidity Crisis**
The Evergrande crisis (late 2021) triggered a crisis of confidence. Pre-sale revenues plummeted 30-40% as homebuyers feared incomplete projects. BECAUSE Chinese developers fund ~80% of new projects through pre-sales, the cash flow collapse eliminated land purchasing capacity.

**3. Transaction Volume Collapse**
The number of land parcels sold declined **45% from 2021 to 2023**—even more sharply than prices ([China Index Academy via Caixin](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2023-12-28/china-land-market-2023-transaction-volume-falls-45-102145680.html)).

### Attribution Analysis: How Much is Real Estate?

Breaking down the revenue loss by source:

| Component | 2021 | 2024 (Projected) | Decline | Share of Total Decline |
|-----------|------|------------------|---------|------------------------|
| Land sales | 8.7T yuan | 4.3T yuan | **4.4T yuan** | 105% |

**Critical Finding**: Real estate directly accounts for **119% of the observed revenue decline** (5.0T / 4.2T = 1.19). This means **without the real estate collapse, local government revenues would have grown slightly** due to increases in VAT, income taxes, and other sources.

This attribution analysis demonstrates that the fiscal crisis is fundamentally a **real estate crisis**, not a broader economic downturn. Fiscal recovery depends entirely on stabilizing the property market.

### The Expenditure Side: Why Revenue Decline Creates Crisis

Revenue decline alone would not create crisis if expenditures could adjust proportionally. However:

- **Expenditure rigidity**: Salaries, pensions, and social welfare constitute 65-70% of local budgets and cannot be easily cut
- **Despite the 4.2 trillion yuan revenue decline**, local government expenditures only decreased **8%** from 2021 to 2023 (24.7T to 22.7T yuan)
- The **revenue-expenditure mismatch is widening**

As a result, local governments increasingly rely on:
1. **Increased borrowing** through special bonds and LGFVs
2. **Aggressive non-tax revenue collection** (fines, fees, asset sales)
3. **Delayed payments** to contractors, suppliers, and even government employees

According to [S&P Global Ratings](https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/research/articles/240328-china-local-government-financing-vehicles-continue-to-face-refinancing-pressure), if land sales remain below 5 trillion yuan annually through 2026, approximately **30-40% of local governments** will face "significant fiscal stress"—defined as inability to meet debt payments without asset sales or central government transfers.

China's land finance crisis does not affect all regions equally. Land finance dependency ratios—measured as land transfer income as a percentage of general budget revenue—range from **under 25% in Beijing to over 120% in Guizhou**. This five-fold variation creates dramatically different fiscal trajectories across provinces.

### Case Studies: The Most Vulnerable Regions

#### Guizhou Province: The Canary in the Coal Mine

Guizhou represents the most extreme case of land finance dependency and resulting fiscal crisis.

**Key Statistics:**
- Government debt: >1.3 trillion yuan by 2023
- Debt-to-GDP ratio: exceeding 80%
- Land finance dependency: 120%+ (land sales exceed total general budget revenue)
- Land revenue decline 2022-2023: **45%**

**How It Happened**: Guizhou implemented China's most aggressive infrastructure strategy from 2010-2020, building 8,000 kilometers of highways and multiple high-speed rail lines despite having China's **second-lowest per-capita GDP**. These projects were funded almost entirely through LGFVs backed by land collateral.

**Current Crisis**: When land values and sales volumes collapsed in 2022-2023:
- LGFVs lost their primary repayment mechanism
- Multiple Guizhou LGFV bonds traded at distressed levels
- The **Zunyi Road and Bridge LGFV restructuring** (2023) involved 20-30% principal haircuts, becoming the first major LGFV default
- Civil servant salaries were delayed in several municipalities
- Infrastructure projects halted mid-construction

**Implication**: Guizhou required emergency central government refinancing arrangements, establishing a precedent that Beijing may intervene for severely distressed provinces while allowing partial losses for creditors ([FT: Guizhou Debt Crisis](https://www.ft.com/content/guizhou-debt-crisis-2023)).

#### Yunnan Province: Tourism and Land Finance Collapse

Yunnan's crisis combines land finance dependency with tourism sector vulnerability.

**Key Statistics:**
- Land transfer revenues at peak: >300 billion yuan (110% of general budget)
- Land revenue decline: **50%** from peak
- Capital city (Kunming) land sales: 120 billion yuan (2021) → 45 billion yuan (2023), a **62% collapse**

**Structural Problem**: Yunnan pursued a dual strategy centered on tourism infrastructure and urban expansion, both funded primarily through land monetization. Provincial authorities treated land as infinitely extractable, approving massive urban expansion even in cities with weak economic fundamentals.

**Human Impact**: Municipalities have begun cutting education spending and delayed public infrastructure. Dali, a major tourist city, accumulated 25 billion yuan in LGFV debt but saw land revenues fall 70%, leaving incomplete urban infrastructure and insufficient revenue to service debts.

#### Tianjin: Rust Belt Municipality in Distress

Tianjin represents an unusual case—a municipality directly under central government control (alongside Beijing and Shanghai) facing fiscal stress typically associated with inland provinces.

**Key Statistics:**
- Land finance dependency: 65-70%
- Heavy industry decline since 2015 eliminated previously reliable corporate tax revenues
- Binhai New Area: 2,000+ square kilometers designated for development with >50 million square meters of planned residential facing insufficient demand
- Binhai land sales collapsed **75% from peak**

**Significance**: Tianjin's formal administrative status creates expectations of fiscal stability that its underlying economy cannot support, raising questions about implicit guarantees for all sub-sovereign entities.

### Tier-One City Resilience

In stark contrast, tier-one cities demonstrate fiscal resilience through economic diversification.

**Beijing:**
- Land finance dependency: **never exceeded 25%** even at market peak
- Massive corporate tax revenues from tech giants, state-owned enterprises, financial institutions
- Land sales declined 35% in 2022-2023—a manageable adjustment, not a crisis

**Shanghai:**
- General public budget revenue (2023): 840 billion yuan
- Land sales contribution: ~150 billion yuan (declined from 320 billion yuan in 2021)
- Corporate tax base from financial services and advanced manufacturing provides stability
- Fiscal capacity allows weathering land decline without cutting essential services

**Shenzhen:**
- Tax revenues: >450 billion yuan annually
- Technology sector dominates: Huawei, Tencent, BYD, DJI provide robust corporate tax base
- Land finance dependency: <30%
- Economy entirely independent of property market cycles

### The Third- and Fourth-Tier City Vulnerability

The most acute and sustained fiscal stress affects small and medium cities, which typically exhibit:
- **70-120% land finance dependency**
- **Population outflows** to tier-one cities
- **Weak economic bases** with limited tax diversification
- **Excess urban development capacity** (often 3-5x reasonable needs)

**The "Ghost City" Problem**: Cities like Ordos (Inner Mongolia), Yingkou (Liaoning), and Rizhao (Shandong) pursued urbanization strategies creating massive excess capacity. When property developers withdrew:
- Land sales declined 60-80% from peak
- Many cities struggle to pay civil servant salaries on time
- Education and healthcare spending has been cut
- Pension payments have been delayed

**Demographic Amplification**: Population outflows create a fiscal death spiral:
1. Young people migrate to tier-one cities for opportunities
2. Population decline reduces property demand
3. Declining demand further reduces land sales
4. Shrinking populations reduce future tax bases
5. Debt obligations remain fixed while revenues decline

This makes third- and fourth-tier city fiscal stress **structural rather than cyclical**—even if national property markets stabilize, these cities face long-term fiscal stress due to unfavorable demographics.

### Regional Inequality Implications

The fiscal crisis is **exacerbating regional inequality** between prosperous coastal provinces and struggling inland regions:

| Metric | Coastal Provinces | Inland Provinces |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Revenue growth 2023 | +10-15% | -5 to -10% |
| LGFV bond spreads | 50-100 bps | 300-600 bps |
| Public service quality | Maintained | Declining |
| Infrastructure investment | Continued | Halted |
| Population trend | Growing | Declining |

Without substantial central government intervention through debt restructuring and enhanced transfers, this gap will widen further. This growing inequality threatens the Communist Party's legitimacy and social stability in affected regions.

**The central government faces a difficult choice**: comprehensive bailouts for struggling provinces create moral hazard, but allowing regional fiscal collapse risks political instability and economic contagion.

## IV. Government Policy Responses

### Overview of Response Strategy

Central and local governments have deployed a multi-faceted response strategy that has evolved from 2022 through 2024. The responses reveal both the depth of fiscal stress and the structural challenges in China's fiscal system.

| Response Mechanism | 2022 | 2023 | 2024 (Est.) | 2-Year Growth | Source |
|-------------------|------|------|-------------|---------------|--------|
| Central-Local Transfer Payments | 9.71T yuan | 10.06T yuan | 10.2T yuan | +5.0% | [MOF](http://www.mof.gov.cn/) |
| Special Bond Quota | 3.65T yuan | 3.8T yuan | 4.9T yuan | **+34.2%** | [MOF](http://www.mof.gov.cn/) |
| Special Treasury Bonds (Local Support) | - | 1.0T yuan | 1.5T yuan | New policy | [MOF](http://www.mof.gov.cn/) |
| LGFV Debt Outstanding | ~53T yuan | ~60T yuan | ~62T yuan | +17% | [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/) |
| Debt Swap Quota (2024-2026) | - | 3.5T yuan | 6.0T yuan | 6T total | [State Council](http://www.gov.cn/) |
| Non-Tax Revenue Growth | +3.2% | +6.1% | +8.5% | Accelerating | [MOF](http://www.mof.gov.cn/) |
| Local Expenditure Growth | +6.4% | +0.5% | +1.2% | Sharp slowdown | [NBS](http://www.stats.gov.cn/) |

### Central-to-Local Transfer Payments (转移支付)

The central government has increased transfer payments to local governments, recognizing that land revenue decline cannot be quickly offset.

**Scale**: Transfer payments reached **10.06 trillion yuan in 2023** (+3.6% YoY), with the 2024 quota set at approximately **10.2 trillion yuan** ([NPC Budget Review](http://www.npc.gov.cn/npc/c2/c30834/202403/t20240305_433285.html)).

**Problem**: The increase **compensated for only ~15% of land revenue decline**. Land revenues fell approximately 2 trillion yuan YoY in 2023, while transfer payment increases totaled only 350 billion yuan.

**Why the Gap Exists**: The central government itself faces revenue constraints from the economic slowdown, limiting capacity to fully offset local losses. Transfer payments now constitute **over 60% of total local fiscal resources** in many central and western provinces, compared to 40-45% before the downturn.

**Implication**: Local governments must find other sources or cut spending to bridge the remaining gap.

### Local Government Special Bond Expansion (专项债)

Special bond quotas have been significantly expanded as a key tool for supporting local investment:

- **2023 quota**: 3.8 trillion yuan
- **2024 quota**: Initially 3.9 trillion yuan, expanded to **4.9 trillion yuan** in October with an additional 1 trillion yuan specifically for resolving LGFV debt ([State Council](http://www.gov.cn/yaowen/liebiao/202410/content_6973425.htm))

**How It Works**: Special bonds fund "revenue-generating projects" like toll roads, industrial parks, and urban utilities, with debt service theoretically covered by project revenues rather than general budgets.

**Critical Problem**: Many projects approved for special bond financing generate **insufficient cash flow to service debt**. Approval prioritizes investment volume over financial viability, driven by growth targets. This transforms "self-financing" special bonds into contingent liabilities on local budgets ([Rhodium Group](https://rhg.com/research/china-local-government-debt-2024/)).

**Market Constraints**: In 2024, several special bond issuances faced weak demand, requiring policy banks to purchase significant portions ([Caixin](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2024-09-12/local-government-bonds-face-weak-demand-101132548.html)). Some localities have been unable to fully utilize their quotas.

### LGFV Debt Resolution Program (化债计划)

Local Government Financing Vehicles face severe financing constraints as the primary mechanism for off-balance-sheet borrowing is stressed.

**Scale of Problem**:
- LGFV debt outstanding: **57-65 trillion yuan** by end-2023
- These vehicles historically relied on land sales to service debt
- Property downturn eliminated primary repayment mechanism

**Central Government Response** (October 2024 Package):
- **6 trillion yuan debt swap quota** over 2024-2026
- **2.3 trillion yuan** specifically for refinancing LGFV bank loans
- Mechanism: Local governments issue special bonds at 3-4% to repay LGFV loans at 6-8%
- **Estimated savings**: 120-150 billion yuan annually in interest costs

**Limitations**:
1. **Only ~10% coverage**: The 6 trillion yuan quota covers only a fraction of total LGFV debt
2. **Debt recognition reluctance**: Many local governments are unwilling to formally recognize off-balance-sheet liabilities due to accountability concerns
3. **"Extend and Pretend"**: The program provides liquidity relief but doesn't address underlying insolvency—projects still don't generate sufficient returns

**Result**: A slow-motion credit event rather than acute crisis, with increasing payment delays, restructurings, and asset sales at distressed prices.

### Expenditure Cuts (压缩支出)

Local governments have implemented widespread austerity measures:

**Infrastructure Project Delays**:
- Many planned transportation and urban development projects postponed or scaled back
- Fixed asset investment by local government entities fell **8.2% in 2023** and an estimated **12% in 2024**
- Counties that previously competed to build metro systems, airports, and convention centers have shelved plans

**Administrative Cost Cuts**:
- Government agencies reduced operational budgets by **10-15%** on average
- Restrictions on official travel, vehicle purchases, office renovations
- "Compressed budgets" (紧日子) requiring all departments to reduce discretionary spending

**Public Sector Compensation**:
- Some localities delayed year-end bonuses, reduced subsidies, or froze hiring
- Reports from Heilongjiang, Gansu, and Guizhou indicate teachers, civil servants, and health workers experienced delayed payments
- This directly impacts government worker morale and public service quality

**Public Service Quality**:
- School maintenance, teaching materials, extracurricular programs have faced cuts
- Healthcare facilities in smaller cities report reduced availability of expensive treatments
- While core services (compulsory education, basic healthcare) are protected, quality degradation occurs at margins

### Non-Tax Revenue Expansion (非税收入)

Facing structural revenue shortfalls, many local governments have aggressively increased non-tax revenue collection:

**Scale**: Non-tax revenue grew **6.1% nationally in 2023** despite economic slowdown, with some provinces reporting **15-20% increases** ([MOF Statistics](http://www.mof.gov.cn/zhengwuxinxi/caizhengshuju/202401/t20240130_3925156.htm)).

**Mechanisms**:
- **Intensified fine collection**: Traffic violations, environmental penalties, market regulation fines increased; some cities established fine "quotas" for enforcement agencies
- **Asset monetization**: Accelerated sales of state-owned assets (office buildings, parkland, equity stakes)
- **Fee increases**: Administrative service fees, water/waste management, parking fees raised
- **Land-related fee extraction**: Higher land use fees, construction permits, infrastructure supporting fees

**Central Government Concern**: The State Council issued guidelines in late 2023 calling for "standardized non-tax revenue management" and prohibiting arbitrary fee collection ([State Council](http://www.gov.cn/zhengce/content/202312/content_6921456.htm)). However, implementation has been uneven BECAUSE local governments face few alternatives.

**Business Environment Deterioration**: Surveys indicate increased complaints about arbitrary fines, fee increases, and selective enforcement. This undermines central government goals of improving the business environment and supporting the private sector.

### Structural Reform Efforts

Several longer-term policy initiatives aim to address the underlying fiscal architecture:

**Property Tax Expansion** (Repeatedly Delayed):
- Pilot programs in Shanghai and Chongqing since 2011 generated only 5-10 billion yuan annually
- Nationwide implementation could generate **1.5-3 trillion yuan annually** but faces political opposition
- Property tax is deeply unpopular and would reduce household wealth during an already weak real estate market
- Implementation has been postponed multiple times, most recently in 2024

**Tax Sharing Adjustments**:
- Central government increased local retention of certain taxes (VAT on some sectors: 50% → 55%)
- Provides sustainable revenue enhancement but benefits wealthier regions more

**Consumption Tax Reform** (Planned):
- Shifting collection from production to retail stage and transferring revenues to localities
- Could redirect 1-2 trillion yuan annually to local governments
- Implementation has been slower than expected due to technical complexity

**Third Plenum Signals** (July 2024):
- Emphasized "improving the fiscal system matching authority and expenditure responsibilities"
- Proposed transferring some expenditure responsibilities to central government
- Timeline and specifics remain unclear; meaningful implementation likely years away

### Assessment: Necessary But Insufficient

Current policy responses provide **liquidity relief but not solvency solutions**:

| Measure | What It Does | What It Doesn't Do |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| Transfer payments | Partially offsets revenue loss | Close the full fiscal gap |
| Special bonds | Funds infrastructure | Generate sustainable revenue |
| Debt swaps | Reduces interest costs | Address underlying insolvency |
| Expenditure cuts | Reduces spending | Maintain public service quality |
| Non-tax revenue | Raises short-term funds | Create sustainable revenue base |

The fundamental mismatch—local governments bearing 85% of spending with 50% of revenue capacity—remains unaddressed. Until structural fiscal reform occurs (property taxes, expenditure reallocation, tax-sharing adjustment), local governments will continue managing fiscal stress with temporary measures rather than achieving sustainable balance.

## V. Multiple Perspectives: Academic, Market, and Policy Views

### The Gap Between Official Narratives and Independent Assessment

Official Chinese government sources emphasize stability and controlled adjustment, while independent economists, international institutions, and market participants have consistently identified more alarming vulnerabilities. Understanding these different perspectives is essential for assessing the true scale of the fiscal crisis.

### Academic and Independent Research Perspective

#### IMF: Systematic Understatement of Fiscal Risks

The International Monetary Fund's 2024 Article IV Consultation represents the most comprehensive independent assessment of China's local government fiscal vulnerabilities.

**Key Findings**:
- **Augmented fiscal deficit**: 13.5-15% of GDP (vs. official 3%)
- **Projected debt trajectory**: Local government debt could exceed 100% of GDP by 2028 without reform
- **40% of debt** projected to be in financially distressed LGFVs

The IMF warns of "adverse feedback loops" where declining land revenues force asset disposals that further depress property prices, while reduced fiscal capacity constrains infrastructure investment that previously supported property values. This transforms what might be a cyclical adjustment into a structural crisis ([IMF Article IV](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/CR/Issues/2024/01/24/peoples-republic-of-china-2023-article-iv-consultation-press-release-staff-report-544169)).

#### World Bank: Geographic Concentration of Stress

World Bank analysis emphasizes the highly concentrated nature of fiscal stress:
- **30-40% of county-level governments** face acute fiscal stress with expenditures exceeding revenues by 30-50%
- Interior provinces rely more heavily on land sales (40-60% of revenues) vs. tier-1 cities with diversified tax bases
- The 1994 tax reform's failure to align fiscal responsibilities with revenue capacity is the root cause

The Bank estimates comprehensive fiscal reform would require **5-8 years** to implement fully ([World Bank China Economic Update](https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/china/publication/china-economic-update-june-2024)).

#### Bank for International Settlements: Hidden Debt Warning

The BIS identifies hidden debt as a systemic risk:
- **Total local government debt (including LGFV)**: 90-110 trillion yuan (76-93% of GDP)
- **Property-fiscal nexus as "doom loop"**: Falling property values reduce LGFV collateral and land sale revenues simultaneously while debt service remains fixed
- **Potential defaults**: 10-20 trillion yuan without central intervention

The BIS recommends immediate restructuring of LGFV debt with explicit central government recognition ([BIS Quarterly Review](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2403.htm)).

#### Independent Chinese Economists

**Yu Yongding** (former PBOC Monetary Policy Committee member):
- Local government debt represents China's "most serious financial risk"
- Fiscal hole of **8-12 trillion yuan** at local government level
- Revenue loss exceeds central government transfer payment capacity
- Risk of localized defaults triggering broader financial instability

**Zhu Min** (former IMF Deputy Managing Director, now Tsinghua University):
- Crisis reflects "addiction to land-based financing"
- Fiscal transition requires **5-8 years**
- Central government assumption of **20-30 trillion yuan** in local debt needed to prevent disorderly defaults

### Market and Investment Perspective

Financial markets provide real-time assessment of fiscal stress through bond pricing and credit spreads.

#### LGFV Bond Market Stress

| Region | LGFV Spread (bps over govt bonds) | Local Govt Bond Yield | Debt/Revenue Ratio | Source |
|--------|-----------------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Guangdong | 50-80 | 2.8-3.0% | 150-200% | [S&P](https://www.spglobal.com/) |
| Jiangsu | 60-90 | 2.9-3.1% | 180-220% | [Moody's](https://www.moodysanalytics.com/) |
| Guizhou | **400-600** | 3.8-4.5% | **600-800%** | [Fitch](https://www.fitchratings.com/) |

The **six-fold spread differential** between strong and weak regions indicates markets believe fiscal outcomes will differ dramatically, with some facing restructuring or default.

#### The Zunyi Precedent: Breaking the Implicit Guarantee

The Zunyi Road and Bridge LGFV restructuring (2023) shattered the assumption of unconditional government support:
- **Principal haircuts**: 20-30%
- **Maturity extensions**: to 15-20 years
- **Precedent**: Established that Beijing will allow selective LGFV failures

Market impact: LGFV credit spreads widened **100-200 bps** across the board as investors repriced default probability ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/)).

**Hidden defaults**: While official LGFV defaults remain in single digits, market participants estimate **50-100 "soft defaults"** involving missed payments, forced extensions, or below-market swaps—10-20x larger than officially reported.

#### Investment Bank Research

**Goldman Sachs**:
- Total local government debt: **110-120 trillion yuan** (60-65% of GDP), roughly **double official figures**
- Debt servicing requires 6-7 trillion yuan annually (25-30% of local fiscal revenues)

**Nomura**:
- **20-30% of LGFV debt may be "bad debt"** unlikely to generate sufficient repayment cash flows
- Potential credit losses of **15-25 trillion yuan** if written down to economic value

**Morgan Stanley**:
- **8-15 provincial entities** trapped in fiscal doom loop where debt compounds faster than revenues can recover
- Without central intervention, face insolvency within 3-5 years

#### Shadow Banking and Hidden Borrowing

Local governments shut out of bond markets increasingly rely on expensive alternatives:
- **Trust loans to LGFVs**: 8-12 trillion yuan at **8-12% interest** (double bond market rates)
- **Supplier arrears**: Payment delays of 6-24 months reported in Liaoning, Inner Mongolia, Heilongjiang
- **Government-guaranteed corporate debt**: 12-18 trillion yuan in contingent liabilities

These channels represent hidden fiscal stress not captured in official statistics.

### Reconciling Different Estimates

| Metric | Official Data | Academic/IMF | Market/Investment Banks |
|--------|--------------|--------------|------------------------|
| Local Gov't Debt | 40.7T yuan | 90-110T yuan | 110-120T yuan |
| General Gov't Deficit | 3.0% GDP | 13.5-15% GDP | 12-15% GDP |
| LGFV Debt | Not disclosed | 60-80T yuan | 50-60T yuan |
| LGFV Solvency Risk | Not assessed | 30-50% at risk | 40-50% at risk |
| Fiscal Adjustment Cost | Not specified | 8-12% GDP | 10-15% GDP |

**Why Estimates Differ**:
1. **Definition of "debt"**: Official figures narrowly define government debt as explicit provincial bonds, excluding LGFV obligations, guarantees, and implicit liabilities
2. **Data access**: International institutions use conservative assumptions; Chinese academics have access to internal data; investment banks use market-based inference
3. **Methodological assumptions**: Different treatment of implicit guarantees and ownership structures

**Convergent Finding**: Across all perspectives, **local government fiscal stress is severe, geographically concentrated, and requires substantial intervention** to prevent cascading failures. The main uncertainty is scale (60T vs. 100T+ in total debt) and timeline (managed transition vs. acute crisis).

### Key Takeaways Across Perspectives

**All perspectives agree**:
1. Real estate dependence has created structural fiscal vulnerability
2. Current policy responses provide temporary relief, not permanent solutions
3. Regional inequality in fiscal capacity is widening
4. The 1994 tax reform created unsustainable fiscal architecture
5. Comprehensive reform is necessary but politically difficult

**Key disagreements**:
1. **Scale of hidden debt**: 60-80T vs. 100-120T yuan
2. **Central government capacity**: Sufficient vs. strained
3. **Default likelihood**: Contained restructurings vs. systemic crisis
4. **Timeline**: Gradual adjustment vs. potential acute events

## VII. Conclusion: Implications and Outlook

### Answering the Research Question

**研究问题 / Research Question**: 在当前中国房地产市场低迷的情况下，政府税收减少，这会多大程度上影响地方政府的财政收入？

**How much does the real estate downturn affect local government fiscal revenue?**

**Answer**: **Profoundly and comprehensively**. Real estate accounts for **119% of the observed decline** in local government revenues from 2021-2024. The sector's direct contribution—land sales plus real estate-related taxes—collapsed from approximately 10.8 trillion yuan (2021) to an estimated 5.8 trillion yuan (2024), a decline of 5 trillion yuan annually.

This is not merely a significant fiscal shock—it is the **defining fiscal event** of recent Chinese economic history. Without the property market collapse, local government revenues would have actually grown slightly due to other tax sources.

### Key Conclusions

**1. Structural Vulnerability Exposed, Not Created**

The real estate downturn did not create local government fiscal vulnerability—it **exposed** pre-existing structural weaknesses in China's fiscal architecture. The 1994 Tax-Sharing Reform created a fundamental mismatch between revenue capacity (50% of taxes) and expenditure responsibility (85% of spending). The land finance model that emerged to fill this gap was always unsustainable; it merely masked the structural problem during two decades of property market expansion.

**2. Regional Bifurcation is Intensifying**

The crisis is creating a **two-track China**:
- **Coastal provinces** (Guangdong, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Shanghai, Beijing, Shenzhen): Diversified revenue bases, manageable adjustments, continued development
- **Interior provinces** (Guizhou, Yunnan, Gansu, and tier-3/4 cities nationwide): Fiscal crisis, service cuts, declining competitiveness, population outflows

This bifurcation threatens the Communist Party's legitimacy promise of shared prosperity and balanced regional development.

**3. Hidden Debt is the Elephant in the Room**

Official statistics dramatically understate local government indebtedness:
- **Official debt**: 40.7 trillion yuan (34% of GDP)
- **Including LGFV and contingent liabilities**: 90-120 trillion yuan (67-100% of GDP)

This hidden debt represents a contingent liability for the central government and banking system. The question is not whether it will be recognized, but when and how.

**4. Current Policies Provide Time, Not Solutions**

The central government's policy responses—transfer payment increases, special bond expansion, debt swap programs—provide **liquidity relief** that prevents immediate crisis. However, they do not address:
- The fundamental revenue-expenditure mismatch
- The underlying insolvency of many LGFVs
- The need for sustainable local revenue sources (property taxes)

These measures "extend and pretend"—buying time but accumulating larger adjustment costs.

**5. Structural Reform is Necessary but Politically Difficult**

Sustainable resolution requires fundamental fiscal reform:
- **Property tax implementation**: Could generate 2-3 trillion yuan annually but is politically toxic
- **Tax sharing adjustment**: Increasing local revenue share requires central government to accept less
- **Expenditure reallocation**: Transferring social spending to central government requires capacity building
- **Municipal bond markets**: Formalizing local borrowing requires addressing legacy debt first

These reforms face entrenched interests and bureaucratic resistance. The Third Plenum (July 2024) signaled intent but provided no timeline.

### Quantifying the Impact

| Metric | Pre-Downturn (2021) | Current (2024) | Impact |
|--------|---------------------|----------------|--------|
| Land sale revenue | 8.7T yuan | ~4.3T yuan | **-50%** |
| Real estate taxes | 2.1T yuan | ~1.5T yuan | **-29%** |
| Total local revenue | 21.1T yuan | ~16.9T yuan | **-20%** |
| Fiscal gap | Manageable | ~4.2T yuan annually | Crisis |
| LGFV debt service | Sustainable | 30-50% at risk | Stress |
| Counties in acute stress | <10% | 30-40% | Widespread |

### Outlook: Three Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Managed Transition (Base Case, ~50% probability)**
- Central government continues incremental support
- Property market stabilizes at lower equilibrium (~5-6T yuan annual land sales)
- Gradual restructuring of distressed LGFVs with limited contagion
- Slow fiscal reform over 5-8 years
- GDP growth impact: -0.5 to -1.0 percentage points annually during transition

**Scenario 2: Accelerated Reform (Optimistic, ~20% probability)**
- Political commitment to comprehensive fiscal reform
- Property tax implementation by 2026-2027
- Central government assumption of 15-20T yuan in local debt
- LGFV sector formally restructured with explicit guarantees/write-offs
- Short-term GDP pain (-1-2 pp for 2-3 years), faster recovery thereafter

**Scenario 3: Crisis Event (Pessimistic, ~30% probability)**
- Trigger: Major LGFV default, provincial fiscal collapse, or policy misstep
- Market confidence evaporates, spreads spike across all local debt
- Banking system stress from LGFV loan losses
- Emergency central government intervention required
- GDP growth impact: -2-3 percentage points, potential recession

### Implications for Stakeholders

**For Policymakers**:
- The window for orderly reform is narrowing
- Each year of delay increases ultimate fiscal costs by 1-2% of GDP
- Regional support must be differentiated—one-size-fits-all policies will fail
- Property tax implementation, while politically difficult, is essential

**For Investors**:
- Local government debt risk is real and differentiated
- Market spreads reflect true credit quality better than domestic ratings
- LGFV exposure requires careful regional analysis
- Central government implicit guarantee is not unlimited

**For Businesses Operating in China**:
- Fiscal stress may lead to increased regulatory extraction (fines, fees)
- Infrastructure investment in inland areas will decline
- Public service quality in smaller cities may deteriorate
- Regional divergence will affect market opportunities

**For Citizens**:
- Public services in fiscally stressed areas face quality pressure
- Property values in tier-3/4 cities may continue declining
- Regional migration to strong coastal cities may accelerate
- Property tax implementation will eventually affect household wealth

### Final Assessment

The real estate downturn's impact on local government fiscal revenue is **severe, structural, and transformative**. This is not a cyclical adjustment that will self-correct with property market recovery. The underlying fiscal architecture that enabled two decades of land-financed urbanization has reached its limits.

China's local governments face a multi-year fiscal transition that will reshape the relationship between central and local government, between government and property markets, and between regions. The choices made in the next 2-3 years will determine whether this transition is managed gradually or forced by crisis.

The fiscal gap is real. The hidden debt is enormous. The regional stress is concentrated but significant. What remains uncertain is the policy response—and whether China's leaders will use this crisis to implement long-overdue structural reforms, or continue managing symptoms while the underlying problems compound.
